About

I'm a physical geographer with a particular interest in how the Earth's surface interacts with electromagnetic radiation — from the spectrometer readings I take lying in a New Mexico grassland to the satellite pixels those readings eventually help calibrate. My undergraduate work at Manchester ranged across GIS, remote sensing, glaciology, hydrochemistry, soil science, and economic geography: the full width of a discipline that refuses to stay indoors.

My doctoral research at Swansea, supervised by M.J. Barnsley, sits at the intersection of satellite radiometry and biophysical modelling. I work with data from POLDER on ADEOS and MODIS on Terra — instruments that were either newly launched or still in development when I started — inverting bidirectional reflectance distribution function (BRDF) models to retrieve land surface albedo and vegetation canopy properties at continental scale. The field campaigns in Norfolk and New Mexico are where I go to check whether the models are telling the truth.

I care about the gap between what satellites see and what is actually on the ground. Closing that gap requires both a spectrometer and a working knowledge of radiative transfer physics. I try to be useful at both ends.

PhD Research · 1995–2000 · Core

BRDF inversion for land surface parameter retrieval — POLDER/ADEOS and MODIS

Swansea · Norfolk · Jornada del Muerto, New Mexico
Situation MODIS on Terra and POLDER on ADEOS were newly operational instruments capable of observing land surface reflectance across multiple view angles simultaneously — more information than any single-view sensor had ever captured. The theoretical framework for inverting this multi-angle data into physically meaningful surface properties (albedo, leaf area index, fraction of absorbed photosynthetically active radiation) existed but had not been operationalised at global scale. Ground truth data to validate the inversion products was sparse and poorly standardised. Task As a doctoral researcher within the Swansea Earth Observation group led by Prof. M.J. Barnsley, I was responsible for field campaign design and execution, development of validation methodology, and contributing to the inversion algorithm development that fed into what became the operational MODIS BRDF/Albedo product (MOD43). Action I designed and ran field campaigns at two contrasting sites: agricultural Norfolk (heterogeneous, temperate, well-documented) and the Jornada del Muerto semidesert in New Mexico (sparse vegetation, high reflectance, PROVE '97 campaign site). At both, I collected broadband albedo measurements using paired pyranometers, field spectroradiometry, and canopy structural measurements using the BPMS system. I wrote C and IDL code to process raw field data, match it to satellite overpass geometry, and compare ground-derived albedo to MODIS-retrieved values across spatial scales. I also co-developed graphical teaching tools in Tcl/Tk to support optical remote sensing education at Swansea. Result Field data contributed to validation of the first operational MODIS BRDF and albedo science data products, published in Remote Sensing of Environment. Co-authored papers on spatial variability of albedo in semidesert environments and on the potential of next-generation sensors (CHRIS/PROBA, SPOT-4 VGT) for biophysical parameter retrieval. The work sits at 3,906 citations with an h-index of 7 — primarily because the MODIS product papers became reference literature for the remote sensing community.

Undergraduate Research · 1994 · HE David Travel Bursary

Alpine glacier mass balance and meltwater hydrochemistry

Zermatt, Switzerland · University of Manchester Alpine Glacier Project
Situation Alpine glaciers in the 1990s were beginning to show measurable retreat. The Manchester Alpine Glacier Project operated summer field campaigns to track mass balance, proglacial hydrochemistry, and glacial geomorphology in the Zermatt region — one of the most intensively studied glacial environments in Europe. Task Awarded the HE David Travel Bursary for European research, I joined the project as a student field scientist responsible for stake network measurements, meltwater sampling, and basic GIS data capture of surface features. Action Conducted ablation stake readings across the glacier surface at weekly intervals to quantify mass loss. Collected meltwater samples at proglacial stream sites for hydrochemical analysis (major ion chemistry, suspended sediment load). Mapped surface features using early GPS and transferred data to Arc/Info for spatial analysis back in Manchester. Result Contributed field datasets to the broader project record. The experience of standing on a glacier with a tape measure while knowing the physics of what I was measuring shaped how I subsequently approached satellite validation work — the instinct that models need to be checked by someone willing to go and look.

Public Event · 1994 · Student-Led

Co-operative Conference on Sustainability

Manchester · Co-operative Bank sponsorship
Situation In the mid-1990s, sustainability was not yet mainstream academic vocabulary. A group of Manchester geography students wanted to convene a serious public conversation about it — not an internal seminar, but an open, professionally run day conference that could draw academics, practitioners, and the public together. Task As a member of the student organising committee, I was involved in programme development, speaker coordination, and logistics. We secured sponsorship from the Co-operative Bank — one of the few financial institutions in the UK at the time with an explicit ethical policy — to fund the event. Action Coordinated speaker invitations and briefings, managed session scheduling, and contributed to the public-facing communications for the event. The conference was open to anyone — deliberately not just for students or academics. Result Several hundred attendees across the day. For a student-organised event with no institutional budget, that was a genuine public reach. It taught me that good ideas about complex systems can draw a crowd if you trust the audience's intelligence.

Conference Organisation · 1999

Remote Sensing Society Annual Conference — RSS'99

Cardiff, Wales
Situation The Remote Sensing Society annual conference is the principal UK gathering for the remote sensing research community — academic researchers, satellite operators, government agencies, and commercial users. RSS'99 was hosted in Cardiff. Task As a PhD student within the Swansea Earth Observation group, I contributed to the local organising effort for the conference — the kind of institutional support that makes a conference run without anyone noticing it needs to. Action Involved in logistics, venue coordination, and session support across the conference programme. Also presented research from the MODIS validation work. Result Conference ran successfully. More usefully for me: spending time inside a professional conference as an organiser rather than just an attendee is a different education. You see how a field actually works — what gets discussed in corridors that doesn't make it into papers.
